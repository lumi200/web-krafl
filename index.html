What is Web Typography...and why does it matter?

What is Web Typography? Well...some call it a technique, some call it art, and I call it underrated. Simply put, it is the art, design, and arrangement of text. But why is it underrated? Because if it works, you won't notice it. If it doesn't, you will be repulsed and leave the website with an uneasy feeling.

Web Typography determines how text looks to the user, the reader of a website. It's not only about choosing the right font, but also about spacing, text layout, and many more details. These details define the tone and vibe of your project. Do you know any website or application that has no text on it? Text is all over your website - and Typography is the clothes your words wear. So don't think ever again that good Typography Design consists only of choosing the right font. Rightly chosen fonts improve the readability of your website, or make it unreadable! And the same can be said about the uniqueness of your website - taking overused fonts may let your website look very bland - do you want that? So choosing fonts is not simply choosing fonts after all!

What do you have to consider when choosing Fonts?

"That font looks neat; let's choose that." If you believe this is the right approach, I'm sorry to disappoint you.

Certainly, that's not the way to go. Fonts are more than just about the looks. But don't worry, here is a checklist for you to determine what's important when choosing a font:

Bad Typeface?

Rule No.1: There are no bad typefaces, only badly used typefaces.

Text is not Text

Text can be categorized into three types:

  • Display text: Short and large text, such as titles, should catch the reader's attention and define the vibe of your project.
  • Body text: Main content text. Good body text should be readable and not draw too much attention.
  • Functional text: Used for navigation elements, labels, etc., and should work well in small sizes.

What's the main use?

Consider the purpose of your text:

  • If people will read a lot on your website, focus on a good readable body size typeface.
  • If it needs to draw attention with minimal text, focus on striking headlines.

Who is your audience?

Often overlooked but important – how accessible is your typeface? Does your typeface affect people with disabilities in a negative way? Does it fit well on phones, and what about dark mode?

Weight and Style

Weights of a typeface describe the thickness variations. Consider:

  • If you only need it for headings, one bold weight might be enough.
  • If you need body text, you'll need a regular weight and perhaps another for emphasis.
  • For abbreviations, think about typefaces with "small-cap" support.
  • For tiny text, look for medium weights with sturdy strokes.
  • Check if your desired typeface supports basic punctuation.

Language Support

Ensure the typeface supports all characters of the language you're using. Avoid issues by searching for fonts by language (Google Fonts).

Special Characters

Do you need extras like special characters, symbols, and alternative letters? Look for OpenType-supported typefaces.

Can we afford it?

Most good typefaces aren't free, and if they are, they're often overused. Investing in unique typefaces means investing in the uniqueness of your project.

How fonts feel and pair

While fonts can be categorized into specific, applying them strictly might not always give a great result. Many typefaces blur the line between those categories. Rather than follow the categories and their use cases, you should look at a font separately and experiment what fits best for your project. Still, the categories and what feeling they convey might be helpful to start

Sans Serif

Sans Serif looks modern, looks simple, minimal and clean. Usually, all strokes of the Font are the same width. Sans-Serif fonts are the most used category when in comes to the web. They are fine if you need them for long text bodies, for a user interface or as Headings or as Headings

Serif

Serif typefaces have tapers on them and have been used since the 15th century in printing. These fonts give a warm, traditional and trustworthy feeling. They are good for headings and long text bodies, but shouldn't be used in an interface.

Slab Serif

The Slab Serif fonts are less smooth and have more hard edges. They were invented in the 19th Century and look "wild western". They give of a stable, confident and clunky feeling. Same as the Serif fonts, they can be used for text bodies and headings, but shouldn't be used in an interface

Script

Script fonts are the opposite of simple and clean, they are inspired by calligraphy typefaces. The category for this typeface is very broad and includes many styles. This typeface should exclusively be used for headings. Because else, readability suffers a lot.

Handwritten

While script fonts try to be fancy, handwritten fonts try to be relatable and "normal" and give of a human, friendly and authentic feeling. Same as the script fonts, this should only be used as a heading. While not as difficult to read as most script fonts, it's still harder compared to other fonts

Display

Display fonts, to simplify, is everything that doesn't fit in a category above. These typefaces can be very atmospheric. Since they can look very different, they don't give of any specific feeling, rather they strike in their unique. Still, they should only be used in a heading.
